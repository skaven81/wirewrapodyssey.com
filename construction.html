Wire Wrap Construction

The Odyssey computer is built entirely on Augat wire wrap prototyping boards.

Why wire wrap?

To answer this, let's look at why not some of the alternatives.

Breadboards

Breadboards are inexpensive and very accessible. All the wiring is on the top side, so it's easier to get the wiring right, particuarly when prototyping. And with custom-cut solid core wire and some clever wire routing, bread board designs can be quite elegant and reasonably simple to maintain. Here's a good example of an 8-bit CPU on a breadboard. It doesn't look too bad.

However, there are lots of downsides with breadboards for a project like this, that relegates breadboard-based designs to the "toy" category.

  • Breadboards have very low component density. Look at the photos in the link above. the whole computer is nearly as large as the Odyssey, but only has around 50 chips. By comparison, the Odyssey has nearly 150 chips! Wire wrap prototyping boards are 2-4x denser than breadboards.
  • Breadboards have a lot of parasitic capacitance. This severely limits signaling speeds, and thus clockspeed. Breadboard CPUs tend to run well under 1MHz clock speeds to stay stable. Wire wrap prototyping boards have solid power and ground planes and high quality pin connections. Decoupling capacitors are easy to bind to a nearby ground. The Odyssey runs just fine at nearly 3MHz, and is limited mostly by signal propagation than parasitic capacitance.
  • The connections on a breadboard are spring-fit and do not hold the wires particularly tightly. Working on a breadboard with lots of wires can often cause some wires to get inadvertently pulled out. Additionally, corrosion can cause poor connections, especially in the cheaper breadboards that don't have gold plated terminals. Wire wrap, on the other hand, is incredibly reliable. According to Wikipedia, Wire wrap construction can produce assemblies that are more reliable than printed circuits: connections are less prone to fail due to vibration or physical stresses on the base board, and the lack of solder eliminates soldering faults such as corrosion, cold joints and dry joints. The connections themselves are firmer and have lower electrical resistance due to cold welding of the wire to the terminal post at the corners.

Printed Circuit Boards

PCBs is how all the cool kids are prototyping these days. With shops like PCBWay able to turn out professionally printed circuit boards for just a few tens of dollars, it makes perfect sense to take advantage of the reliability, speed, and quality of a PCB when building a big complex project like this. So why did I use wire wrap instead?

Well, the biggest disadvantage (for me, at least) with using PCBs is that everything has to be designed upfront. The whole computer has to be designed and laid out using CAD software, then sent off to the PCB shop, and then the boards come back and get assembled and tested. It's efficient, but for me, it doesn't sound like a lot of fun. Since I built the Odyssey as a hobby project, I didn't want to have to worry about having everything all decided up front. I had a lot more fun just throwing chips onto a board and hacking on it for a weekend to get an idea to work (or not). That's not possible with PCB design.

Perfboard / Stripboard

This approach seems like the best balance between durability/reliability and "hack it together" fun of prototyping. And in fact I was so convinced of that when I first started building the Odyssey, that this is precisely what I did. I purchased plated perfboards and would spend hours soldering 30 gauge wires point to point on the back of the board to implement an idea.

This works fine for small projects. But as the complexity grew, it became increasingly daunting to have to solder all these wires. And when soldering, any pins that have mutliple wires attached become extremely difficult to deal with, as every time you melt the solder, the other wire(s) want to move around.

It turns out that our forebears already solved this problem with wire wrap prototyping. It solves the durability/reliability problem while retaining the density of a PCB or perfboard construction. And multiple nets are easy to attach to a single wire wrap pin without disturbing existing wiring. It's even relatively easy to make repairs -- often it's just a matter of cutting out the offending wire (leaving the dead wraps on either end) and wrapping a new wire to fix it. The posts are tall and can accommodate a few mistakes.

Wire Wrap Supplies

Prototyping boards

I gravitated toward professional wire wrap prototyping boards. These were likely hideously expensive when they were originally made and used in the 1970s and 1980s. But these days you can pick them up on eBay for 50-200 dollars. That might sound like a lot compared to a bunch of breadboards, but note that it's a LOT more prototyping space than a breadboard, and the finished product will be a lot more reliable.

On eBay, I find that searching "augat prototyping board" brings up good matches. Augat is a brand name of prototyping board. There are others, but Augat seems to have the best quality boards that I've found.

Prototyping boards will typically have columns of wire wrap posts set 0.3" apart (the standard width of a DIP chip), with 50 pins in each column. There will be a power plane on one side and a ground plane on the other, with wire wrap posts set every 8-10 pins. Most prototyping boards have some kind of external interface -- I'm sure many were designed to plug into a big backplane.

Wire wrapping wire

I have used many different types of wire wrapping wire, but the best by far is 30 gauge Kynar insulated wire. All the big shops like Jameco, Mouser, Digikey, etc. sell it. Just search for "Kynar" and filter to 30 gauge wire.

Wire wrapping tools

The pros back in the day would use a wire wrapping gun like this. I probably should have picked one of these up early on, but instead I've gotten along well with one of these.

I also use a 30 gauge wire stripper, a tiny flathead screwdriver I use for pushing wires around on the board, and a good pair of tweezers.

Wire Wrap Technique

This is probably best taught via a YouTube video. Bil Herd has a great introduction that he did for Hackaday:

Personally, the only tip I can really think of is to not apply too much pressure on the wrapping tool. Only the slightest pressure (barely more than the weight of the tool itself) should be applied to the pin when spinning the tool to wrap the pin. If you apply too much pressure, it causes overwrap, which is not only ugly, it can also lead to broken bits of wire falling off and causing shorts in the project.
